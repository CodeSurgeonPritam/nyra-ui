Color is the cheapest way to be remembered.

Logos take months. Wordmarks take a typographer. A motion system takes a
team. A *single hex value* takes one decision — and from that point forward,
every screenshot of your product carries it.

Nyra's color is `#c8ff3c`. Electric lime on ink black. We tried other things
first. None of them stuck.

## What we tried

**Purple.** Every dev tool is purple. Linear, Vercel's old palette,
half the YC batch from 2022. Putting Nyra in purple was like printing the
logo upside-down — you couldn't tell anything apart.

**Saturated red.** Too aggressive for editorial typography. The serif and
the red fought each other on every page; one of them had to lose.

**Teal.** Too tasteful. Survived one design review and immediately blended
into the background. Memorable colors are slightly *wrong* — they have
something to fight against.

**Lime.** Wrong in the right way. Against ink black, it reads almost
neon — too much so, at first. Against bone (the light theme background),
it calms down. The contrast carries the brand without screaming.

## Where to use it

The hard part of an electric accent is *restraint*. The cheap version of
this aesthetic is "lime everywhere" — every button, every link, every
border. After ten seconds your interface looks like a parking lot at night.

The rule we settled on:

> **Lime is for what you want noticed once.**

In practice:

- The primary CTA. Not every button — the *one* button on the screen that
  matters.
- Hover states. The accent appears when the user is already paying attention.
- Tiny indicators. Active tab. Open status. Selected option.
- Editorial italics. The word the sentence is built around.

Everywhere else: bone on ink, with the muted token doing the work. The
moment lime stops being scarce, it stops being signal.

## The light-mode trap

The hardest part of lime is the light theme. On a dark background, the
contrast is high enough to feel ambient. On a bone background, lime against
bone is barely legible — you can read it, but it doesn't *pop*.

The fix: in light mode, the accent foreground swaps to ink. We keep the
lime fill (so the brand color is the same in both themes), but the text
sitting on top changes from bone to ink. Same color, different role.

That's the whole brand. One hex value, used carefully. Everything else —
the type, the motion, the components — exists to give the lime something
to be loud against.
